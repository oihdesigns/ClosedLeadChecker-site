Blinky Hawk User Guide

Clip it onto the meter you already own. From then on, a 0 V reading always comes with an answer: connected, open, or live.

Blinky Hawk is a small battery-powered detector that piggybacks on your multimeter's voltage inputs. Whenever the voltage across your probes is near zero, it continuously checks whether the leads actually see a circuit — without your meter ever leaving voltmeter mode — and tells you with a flash and a beep.

1 · What it does

Three answers instead of one ambiguous zero

A normal voltmeter reading 0 V can't tell you whether you're on a genuinely connected net, or simply not touching anything — a floating probe, a clip that fell off, an open relay. Blinky Hawk resolves that ambiguity continuously:

Closed
Green flash + beep

The probes see a low-impedance path — your 0 V is a real zero on a connected circuit.

Open
Dim blue flash

The probes are floating — that 0 V means not connected, not "no voltage."

Voltage
Red flash + double beep

A live voltage is present. The continuity check suspends itself automatically until the voltage is gone.

It works by briefly interrupting an internal resistor bridge and watching how the resulting microsecond transient decays across the probes — high impedance decays slowly (open), low impedance decays fast (closed). The injected signal is tiny (never more than ~1.25 V behind hundreds of kilohms, worst-case ~6 µA), your meter stays in voltmeter mode the whole time, and detection runs many times per second. Full technical background is on the main project page.

2 · Quick start

From box to first detection

  1. Plug Blinky Hawk into your meter. Its stackable banana plugs go straight into the meter's V / COM jacks — the same two jacks you use for voltage measurements.
  2. Plug your test leads into the back of Blinky Hawk's plugs. The connection is purely parallel: your probes, your meter, and your workflow are unchanged.
  3. Flip the toggle switch ON. This one switch both powers the unit and connects it to the measurement path (details below).
  4. Watch the power-on cue. The LED blinks green 1–4 times to report the battery level (1 = 0–25%, 4 = 75–100%).
  5. Use your meter normally. Touch the probes together — green flash and a beep. Separate them — dim blue flashes. Probe something live — red flash and a double beep, and the meter shows the voltage as always.

Voltage modes only. Leave your meter in DC or AC volts while Blinky Hawk is on — that's the mode it's designed to live in, and it never asks you to leave it. Before using any other mode (resistance, continuity, diode, capacitance, or current), flip Blinky Hawk OFF: it sits in parallel with the input jacks, and its sense bias can disturb those measurements. OFF makes your meter fully stock again.

3 · The toggle

One switch: power and isolation

The toggle is a triple-pole switch: one pole switches the battery, and the other two galvanically connect/disconnect both measurement leads.

PositionWhat it means
ON Blinky Hawk is powered and connected across the probes. Detection runs continuously; the meter may show a few millivolts of ghost voltage when the leads are open.
OFF Fully inert. The battery is disconnected and the detector is galvanically removed from the probe circuit — your meter behaves 100% stock, with zero ghost voltage and zero battery drain. You can leave Blinky Hawk plugged in permanently and simply switch it off when you don't want it. OFF is also the right position whenever you use the meter's non-voltage modes (Ω, continuity, diode, current). Note that OFF disconnects the battery from the charger too — the unit must be ON to charge.
4 · Indicators

LED & beep reference

LED

PatternMeaning
Green flash (up to 2/s) Leads closed — continuity.
Dim blue flash (up to 1/s) Leads open / floating.
Red flash (up to 2/s) Voltage present — continuity checking suspended.
1–4 green blinks at power-on Battery level in quarters (1 = low, 4 = full).
Slow dim-red blink Charging over USB. Normal alerts are suppressed while charging.
Slow dim-green blink while plugged in Charging, and the battery is effectively full.
Dark between flashes Normal — alerts are brief rate-limited flashes to conserve battery, not steady lights.

Beeps

  • Single beep the moment the leads close, then a short reminder beep about once a second for as long as continuity holds.
  • Double beep (higher pitch) when a voltage is detected. This is a priority alert — it sounds immediately, even mid-continuity-beep.
  • Rate-limited: an intermittent connection can't machine-gun the buzzer; beep sequences are capped at 2 per second.
  • Quiet mode, no tools: hold the probe tips together (or clip them) while flipping the power switch on — audio stays muted until the next power cycle. The LED continues to work normally.
  • Every beep behaviour — including disabling the buzzer permanently — is reconfigurable over USB; see the firmware manual.
5 · Interpreting results

What the verdicts mean at the bench

Probes separated byMeter showsBlinky Hawk says
Copper — same net0 VClosed
Air gap / floating probe0 VOpen
Open relay or switch0 VOpen
GPIO pin driven low0 VClosed
GPIO pin, MCU unpowered0 VOpen
Capacitor (unenergized)0 VOften Closed while it charges *
Transformer winding-to-winding0 VMay read Closed (reactive coupling) *
Anything energizedthe voltageVoltage — check suspended

* The one honest limitation. Blinky Hawk detects a low-impedance path to a fast injected transient. The vast majority of the time that means a genuine wire-to-wire connection — but reactive components are the exception. A capacitor passes the transient and can read "Closed"; a transformer can couple it through interwinding capacitance and read "Closed" even when a megohmmeter shows the windings galvanically wide open. Near capacitors, transformers, and large inductors, treat a green "Closed" as "low-impedance path", not "guaranteed DC connection."

The reverse direction is trustworthy everywhere: a blue "Open" verdict means the probes genuinely see a high impedance — there is no low-resistance connection between them.

6 · Ghost voltage

Why your meter shows a few millivolts with open leads

With Blinky Hawk switched on and the leads open, your meter will typically display a small offset — around 5 mV DC (tens of mV on AC ranges, depending on the meter). This is the detector's tiny sense bias, visible only because nothing else is driving the probes.

  • It is cosmetic, not a measurement error. The bias sits behind very high source resistance, so probing any real circuit pins the reading to the true value.
  • It disappears during real measurements. The instant a voltage is detected, the bridge stops toggling entirely.
  • It's removable. Flip the toggle OFF and the detector is galvanically gone — the meter reads exactly as it would bare.
  • Higher sensitivity settings use more bridge resistance, which increases ghost voltage slightly; the factory setting is chosen to keep it minimal.
7 · Sensitivity

The DIP switches (most users can skip this)

Under the hood, sensitivity is a genuine tradeoff: more bridge resistance produces a stronger detection signal (better discrimination of high-resistance circuits) but slightly more ghost voltage on the host meter. A small 4-way DIP switch on the board lets you pick where on that tradeoff you sit.

  • The four switches work as two pairs that must move together: one pair physically changes the bridge resistance, the other pair tells the firmware which calibrated detection threshold to use. The correct pairing is printed on the PCB next to the switch — always flip a resistance switch together with its marked firmware partner.
  • The factory setting is position 10, tuned for everyday use with minimal ghost voltage. Most users should leave it there.
  • Changes take effect immediately — no reboot, no reprogramming. The unit reports the new position over USB serial if connected.
  • The detection threshold behind each switch position is itself reprogrammable over USB, so a position can be re-purposed for a specific application — see the firmware manual.
8 · Power

Battery & charging

  • Battery: internal single-cell rechargeable lithium battery. (Final capacity is still being selected — expect roughly 150–450 mAh, which at Blinky Hawk's ~11 mA draw works out to very roughly 13–40 hours of continuous ON time.)
  • Zero drain when OFF: the toggle disconnects the battery outright, so a switched-off unit holds its charge indefinitely.
  • Charging — the toggle must be ON: plug a USB-C cable into the port on the unit with the switch in the ON position. The same switch poles that make OFF fully inert also disconnect the battery from the charger, so a switched-off unit ignores USB power entirely. While charging, the LED shows a slow dim-red blink, switching to a slow green blink when the battery is effectively full.
  • Don't use it while charging. Detection alerts (LED and beeper) are deliberately disabled whenever USB power is present, for two reasons: many USB power supplies are electrically noisy enough to cause a stream of false alerts, and the USB cable can couple your meter to an earth ground you may not want in your measurement. Charge it idle, unplug, then work. (For bench and diagnostic sessions the alerts can be forced back on over serial with !ALERTS,1 — see the firmware manual — but the same noise and grounding caveats apply.)
  • Power-on battery gauge: the 1–4 green blinks at every power-up are your fuel gauge; one blink means it's time to charge.
9 · Safety

What it can take, and what to keep in mind

  • It sits beside your meter's inputs. Blinky Hawk connects in parallel across the same jacks your probes use — whatever your probes touch, it sees too. Its front end is built for that exposure: detection has been verified working from 0 V through ±1 kV, including −1 kV applied from a megohmmeter; beyond its sensing range the front end simply saturates behind its own series resistance and clamp protection — there is nothing fragile exposed to the probes.
  • It is gentle on circuits. The injected test signal can never exceed the internal 1.25 V reference, and worst-case current into a dead short is about 6 µA, falling to nano-amps into high impedances — where sensitive electronics would actually care.
  • Your meter's ratings still govern. Blinky Hawk does not change your meter's CAT rating, and it carries no independent safety certification of its own — observe the category and voltage limits of the meter and leads you use, exactly as you would without it.
  • It is an indicator, not a lockout. Use it to catch unplugged-probe mistakes faster — but always verify de-energization per your normal safe work practice before touching a circuit. In particular remember the reactive-parts caveat in section 5.
10 · Troubleshooting

FAQ

SymptomExplanation / fix
Meter shows a few mV with nothing connected Normal ghost voltage while the detector is on and the leads are open. It vanishes on any real measurement; flip the toggle OFF to remove it entirely.
No LED activity at power-on Battery is empty — charge over USB-C. (A single green power-on blink means it was nearly empty.)
LED only does a slow red blink The unit is on USB power and charging; alerts are intentionally disabled while charging (USB supply noise causes false alerts, and the cable can earth-ground your meter). Unplug to resume working.
Plugged into USB but the battery won't charge The toggle must be ON to charge — switching OFF disconnects the battery from the charger as well as from the meter.
No beeps, LED works Audio was muted for this session (leads were touching at power-on) — power-cycle with the leads apart. If it was disabled permanently via configuration, re-enable it (!SET,BEEP,1 + !SAVE).
"Closed" on a part I know is isolated Almost certainly a reactive path — capacitor or transformer interwinding capacitance. See the limitation note in section 5.
Detection seems too eager / not eager enough Try a different DIP position (flip the marked pairs together), or retune the thresholds over USB per the firmware manual.
Meter reads normally but no open/closed verdicts, LED blue Check that both banana plugs are fully seated in the meter's V/COM jacks and the toggle is ON — with the detector isolated the leads always look open.

11 · Advanced

Configuration over USB

Every behavioural setting — the four DIP thresholds, beep pitches and patterns, LED enables, detection timing, battery calibration — is stored in on-board EEPROM and can be changed permanently over the USB-C port with simple text commands (or the companion desktop app), no reflashing required. The complete reference, including a serial-protocol table and step-by-step recipes, is the Blinky Hawk firmware manual.
